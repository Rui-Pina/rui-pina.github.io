I have a friend who is obsessed with money.

We were discussing a headline where Anthropic's CEO, Dario Amodei, said that “50% of all entry-level lawyers, consultants, and finance professionals will be completely wiped out within the next 1–5 years.”

I won’t speculate on whether this is true or false—and I don’t even think Dario himself knows. What interested me was my friend’s reaction.

His response was:

> “Bro, why do you think I’m saving every penny I can? I’m NOT expecting my job to exist in the next 30 years. I’ll probably be jobless for a while at some point.”

Regardless of whether he becomes jobless or not, my answer was simple:

**“So what?”**

---

For months, I’ve been trying to de-brainwash him from the idea that money—essentially just bits stored in someone’s SQL database—is something inherently valuable.

Money has **no intrinsic value**.

Wanting someone else to update a number in their database on your behalf is, at its core, a kind of servile mentality.

So I ask: **who actually has the power here?**

My friend—or the entities printing money at central banks?

---

Central banks can print money indefinitely, bailing out their allies through multi-billion-dollar rescues—like during the 2008 financial crisis.

And what does my friend do?

He tracks every penny he earns and saves all of it.

---

To be clear, I’m not saying he should consume endlessly or indulge in meaningless vices.

I’m not talking about buying Ferraris, Louis Vuitton handbags, or other status symbols. These, too, are largely empty—objects with no real intrinsic value.

We’re even seeing a shift among the wealthy away from material goods toward intangible experiences: front-row seats at Wimbledon, box tickets at Champions League matches, and so on.

But this is just a different form of the same problem.

---

Our society has been deeply conditioned into materialism.

Spirit is gone. Virtue is fading. Everything is permitted.

Worse, society often rewards the most base, impulsive, and destructive behaviors. It feels like an affront to what it means to be human.

---

So what is left?

If it’s not money, and it’s not consumerism—then what is life about?

Some people will immediately answer: **God**.

That’s a valid answer. But I won’t lean on it here, because many people are repelled by that framing.

---

In my view, life is about leaving the world better than you found it.

Take a moment to reflect on the years since you were born.

Has the world improved—or declined?

If you’re a boomer, you might say it’s better. For most other generations, the answer is less clear—perhaps even the opposite.

So the real question becomes:

**How can I make it better?**

---

The answer is simple—at least in principle.

Stop exploiting others.

Stop extracting value from people without giving anything meaningful in return.

Be kind. Help one another. Build things that genuinely improve human life.

Medical devices. Agriculture. Essential manufacturing.

We don’t need another Uber Eats or another Candy Crush.

These are distractions—profitable ones, yes—but ultimately distractions from what we *could* be creating… and from the beauty we could be experiencing.

---

The ultimate aim of life is love.

A loving partner. Raising your children as well as you can. Helping your neighbor. Contributing to something greater than yourself.

Creating something meaningful.

---

Our era calls for something more.

It calls for force-of-nature individuals—the kind we read about in history.

People like Caesar, Themistocles, or Napoleon.

Yes, they did terrible things. That’s not the point.

The point is their **intensity**, their **drive**, their almost prophetic energy.

That is what feels missing today.

And that is what feels needed.
